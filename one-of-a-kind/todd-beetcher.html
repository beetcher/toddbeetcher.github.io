The Story

"There is exactly one place in the United States where a school bus driver does what I did last summer. One city. One route. One person. I know this because I've looked, and because the geography makes it mathematically impossible anywhere else."

Pre-dawn — Boulder to Lakewood

My day started before the kids ever saw a bus. I live in Boulder. The depot is in Lakewood — on the west side of Denver — which means my morning begins with an hour of personal driving just to get to the bus. I'm already in the car before most people have turned off their alarm.

I arrive at the depot. I prep the bus. A full-size 70-passenger school bus. I check it, walk it, make sure everything is right. Then I pull out of Lakewood and into what is already becoming Denver's morning rush hour.

Rush Hour × 2 — Into the City and Out

You almost never see school buses on the freeway. They run suburban routes, neighborhood streets, predictable loops. They are not typically threading through downtown Denver interchanges in morning rush hour with 60 kids on board.

I was out there every Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday. Denver to Boulder is roughly 30 miles on US-36 but in morning rush hour it runs long. I've got 70 kids behind me. I've got a magnetic phone mount on the windshield running a playlist. I've got a Bluetooth speaker system strung through the cabin. We are not sitting in silence. We are running a show.

"Nursery rhymes for the little ones. Pop music. Call and response. By the time we clear Boulder traffic the bus is already a dance party."

The Canyon

We clear Boulder and turn north. Left Hand Canyon Road. This is where the drive changes character entirely. It is narrow in places. It winds. The elevation gain is continuous and significant — you're going from roughly 5,400 feet at the canyon mouth to over 9,000 feet near Ward. The road follows Left Hand Creek. There are no wide shoulders. There are no second chances.

I am driving a full-size 70-passenger school bus up this road. With 70 kids. And no retarder.

The Retarder Problem

A retarder is a braking system designed specifically for large vehicles on mountain grades. It uses engine compression or electromagnetic resistance to slow the vehicle without depending entirely on the service brakes. In Colorado, mountain bus routes are supposed to have them. They are standard equipment for a reason — descending a loaded bus down 17 miles of canyon road on brakes alone generates dangerous heat and wear.

My bus didn't have one. The Boys & Girls Club had purchased a bus that was not spec'd for mountain driving. And so I — a certified mountain driver, CDL with school bus endorsement and mountain certification — drove that bus up and down Left Hand Canyon all summer on service brakes and skill alone. Nobody else on that route had my certification. Nobody else was doing what I was doing.

Why This Route Exists Nowhere Else

The combination of factors that makes this route one of a kind is not subjective. It is geographic and logistical. You need a major metro with significant rush hour congestion — Denver qualifies. You need a secondary city with its own rush hour between the metro and the mountains — Boulder qualifies, and almost nothing else does. You need a mountain destination close enough for a day trip. You need the canyon road itself — narrow, gaining thousands of feet, demanding mountain certification.

Los Angeles has the traffic but the mountains are too far. San Francisco has traffic and mountains but the geography doesn't stack the same way. Denver to Boulder to Left Hand Canyon to Ward is the only place in the country where all of it stacks. I was the only driver doing it.
